Preserving Conservatism: Post-World War II U.S. Recreational Parks as a Memento of Cultural Nationalism

Published

May 1, 2024

David P. Askew

Digital Methods II

Abstract

This project will answer a historical question of whether the purpose of post-World War II recreational parks were a means of preserving conservative norms and to commemorate cultural nationalism. It is certain that by using the digital tool of text analysis, prominence of keywords in magazines featuring these parks like “national,” “nation,” and “patriot” demonstrate this to be the case. The project will also address how one should understand the significance of American patriotism as it relates to recreational parks and the way this concept has evolved as parks have changed over time.

Introduction

The year is 1945 and many soldiers are coming home exhausted and relieved from their duties overseas. It is at this juncture that the United States reached the peak of its influence, and a sense of pride and accomplishment could be felt throughout the country. This pride came with the recognition that both the United States and the world were spared from the influence of Nazi Germany and the Axis alliance. Nazism was understood by many as an ideology which threatened American culture and particularly conservative norms in the U.S. This is true as many of these norms related to patriotism and nationality.
